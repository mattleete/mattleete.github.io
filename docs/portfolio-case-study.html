End-to-end product design for a mobile-first social platform — from research and strategy through to shipped UI and a living design system.
01

The Problem

Spix needed to carve out a distinct position in a crowded social app market. Early user testing revealed that existing platforms left a specific user need unaddressed — the desire for smaller, more intentional social connections without the noise of large public feeds.

The challenge was translating a nuanced social behaviour into a product experience that felt natural and low-friction, while building in the interaction patterns that would drive long-term retention.

Research synthesis from 24 user interviews — affinity mapping session

We ran 24 in-depth user interviews, competitive analysis across 12 platforms, and a 2-week diary study to understand social media behaviour patterns in the target demographic.

02

The Approach

We ran three design sprints over six weeks, moving from problem framing to low-fidelity concepts to testable prototypes. Each sprint ended with usability sessions that fed directly into the next iteration.

Left: Early wireframe concepts. Right: Sprint 2 prototype tested with 8 users.
"It feels like the app actually understands what I want to share, not just what's easy to share."
User interview participant — Sprint 3 testing

A key design decision was the "circle" metaphor — limiting visible audiences by default and requiring active opt-in to share more broadly. This reduced posting anxiety significantly in testing while maintaining the social rewards that drive engagement.

03

The Solution

Final UI — Home feed, profile, and post creation flow

The shipped product introduced a gesture-first navigation system, an opt-in audience model, and a content format built around short media rather than long-form text. The design system built alongside the product gave the engineering team the components and logic needed to ship at pace.

Learning 01
Constraints are your friend
The audience limitation feature felt counterintuitive to stakeholders but was the most positively received feature in testing. Shipping the constraint built trust.
Learning 02
Design system investment pays early
Building the component library from sprint 1 — not after — meant we could prototype at high fidelity within days and the engineering handoff required almost no rework.
Learning 03
Retention is a design problem
Mapping the day-1, day-3, and day-7 user journeys as separate design briefs — not just an onboarding flow — surfaced critical drop-off points that data alone wouldn't have caught.
Learning 04
Test your assumptions, not your solutions
Framing usability sessions around behaviours and mental models rather than feature validation gave us richer insight and surfaced two major pivots before we'd written a line of code.
