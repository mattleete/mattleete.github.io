CX Strategy Journey Design Superannuation 12 Weeks

Reducing Churn at the Moment of Value

Member Experience Strategy & CX Journey Design for a Major Australian Superannuation Fund

My Role
Designer, Strategist & Facilitator
Industry
Superannuation
Duration
12 Weeks
Team Size
3–4 People
Deliverables
CX Journey Map
Experience Framework
Design Concepts
The Challenge
A 2-million-member super fund was losing its highest-potential members at the exact moment they were becoming most valuable — just as they began to accumulate meaningful retirement savings.
The Approach
Over 12 weeks: research synthesis, CX strategy, journey mapping, concept design and user testing — delivering a comprehensive experience framework and actionable design concepts.
The Outcome
A validated experience framework and suite of design concepts received with strong enthusiasm by the client — described as immediately actionable and aligned with their strategic direction.
01

The Problem

The fund had a well-documented retention problem. Members were entering a phase of their financial lives where super began to feel more real and relevant — yet the experience they encountered at that moment was disconnected, generic and difficult to navigate. Rather than deepening their relationship with the fund, this friction pushed them toward competitors.

Two dynamics compounded the issue:

Low Engagement
Members didn't feel a sense of ownership or connection to their super — it was something that happened to them, not something they actively managed. When they did interact, the experience failed to meet the moment.
'Starter Fund' Perception
Many members perceived the fund as a starter option — a choice for people who hadn't yet made a deliberate decision about their super. As their financial lives matured, this made them feel they'd outgrown it.
The Key Signal
Members who logged into the fund's app had significantly lower churn rates than those who didn't. Regular app engagement was one of the strongest behavioural signals of member retention.

The business consequence was measurable. A fund managing tens of billions in retirement savings was losing high-potential members at the exact moment those members were poised to deliver the greatest long-term value.

03

My Role

I operated across three distinct functions throughout the engagement, often switching between them within a single working session.

Strategist
Led the synthesis of existing member research and translated it into a strategic framework. This included identifying the core tension at the heart of the problem — a mismatch between the life stage of at-risk members and the experience the fund was delivering — and developing a point of view on where the greatest leverage lay.
Experience Designer
Designed the end-to-end CX journey map in Figma, visualising the member experience across key moments and touchpoints. Led concept ideation and developed the design concepts that formed the final client deliverable, taking them through rounds of iteration informed by user testing.
Facilitator
Planned and facilitated collaborative workshops with the client team throughout the engagement — aligning stakeholders on strategic direction, pressure-testing journey assumptions, and building shared ownership of emerging concepts. Critical for ensuring the work had legs beyond the consulting engagement.
04

The Member Persona

To ground the strategy and design work, we developed a dual-stage persona representing the at-risk member segment at two critical points in their life. Rather than a single static profile, the persona showed the same person at two different ages — capturing how their relationship with super needed to evolve as their life circumstances changed.

Age 18
First job, no financial context
Grew up in a close-knit regional family with limited exposure to financial planning. After finishing high school, she landed a part-time retail job — her first experience of earning an income. She has no clear career direction, and while she's excited about earning money, adult financial responsibilities feel unfamiliar and slightly overwhelming. Super exists in the background — she knows it's there, but it holds no real meaning for her yet.
Age 21
Career beginning, life getting more complex
Three years later, she's completed a diploma and been offered her first professional role at a youth mental health foundation in Sydney. She's relocating from her hometown, navigating rent, full-time work and city life simultaneously. Financial management is becoming a more pressing reality — but her immediate focus is on making the transition work. Super still isn't a priority, but this is precisely the moment she's most vulnerable to switching and most open to being engaged.
The dual-stage persona was most valuable during strategy and problem framing — helping align the client team around a shared, human understanding of who they were designing for and why the experience was falling short at a critical life moment.

05

Process

The 12-week engagement was structured as a phased progression from understanding through to validated design concepts.

1
Research Synthesis & Strategic Framing
The fund had already invested in qualitative and quantitative member research. Rather than duplicate that effort, we immersed ourselves in the existing body of work — identifying patterns, tensions and unanswered questions. From this synthesis, we developed a clear strategic framing: a definition of the at-risk member segment, a map of key moments where experience was falling short, and a set of design principles to guide the work ahead. A workshop with the client's strategy and product teams stress-tested our synthesis early in this phase.
2
CX Vision, Principles & Journey Mapping
Before mapping the journey, we established a clear experience vision and design principles. The vision: make super surprisingly simple — acknowledging super is inherently complex, and that the goal was to make engaging with it feel effortless and even unexpectedly enjoyable. Three principles underpinned this:
Intuitive
Every interaction should feel natural and require no instruction to navigate.
Personalised
The experience should reflect the member's individual goals, financial literacy and life stage — not a generic template.
Surprising
The fund should create moments of genuine delight in unexpected places, shifting super from obligation to something worth engaging with.

With the vision and principles established, we built the journey map across three key stages:

Stage 01
Acquire & Onboard
Problem
New members have a lot going on. They don't have the time or headspace to engage with super, and begin their journey with apathy or misunderstanding about what it is and why it matters.
Goal
A warm welcome followed by a simple, guided onboarding journey that personalises the member's future experience based on their goals and financial literacy level.
Stage 02
Reasons to Stay
Problem
Young members often lack awareness, interest and understanding of their super. Others perceive the fund as one for beginners — a default choice. Both lead to apathy as they approach a job change.
Goal
Engage younger members by sparking genuine interest in their super, guiding them through priority tasks, and providing consistent reassurance and compelling reasons to return to the app regularly.
Stage 03
Job Change
Problem
Young members lack confidence in the fund as their preferred provider. Many are unaware they can take their super with them when they change jobs. This is the highest-risk moment in the journey.
Goal
Ensure members feel the fund is the right choice at this stage. Provide personalised guidance on how to stay — and a seamless exit experience for those who do choose to leave, leaving the door open for return.
3
Concept Ideation & Design
Working from the opportunity spaces identified in the journey map, we ran structured ideation sessions — both internally and collaboratively with the client. Concepts were designed in Figma to a level of fidelity sufficient for user testing — detailed enough to feel real, but flexible enough to iterate quickly. Each concept was framed around a specific member insight and a clear articulation of the experience shift it was designed to create.
4
User Testing & Refinement
We tested concepts with members representative of the at-risk segment. Testing focused on comprehension, emotional resonance and perceived relevance — we wanted to understand not just whether members understood the concepts, but whether they felt like experiences worth staying for. Several concepts were significantly reworked based on feedback; one concept that had performed strongly in internal reviews was substantially de-prioritised after testing revealed it addressed a problem members didn't actually experience as painful.

06

Deliverables

The final deliverable presented to the client consisted of two core components:

CX Journey Map
A comprehensive, designed artefact mapping the end-to-end experience of the at-risk member segment. The key finding from the current state analysis: the member experience was inconsistent and confusing across touchpoints — members encountered a different look, feel and logic depending on how they engaged with the fund, which eroded confidence and made it harder to take action.
The journey map and accompanying experience framework established a consistent and intuitive experience standard across all touchpoints, and was designed to give members the signals they needed to feel confident — that they had made the right choice and were being looked after well. The framework was designed to be durable beyond the engagement, giving the client's internal teams a shared reference to align future work.
Design Concepts
A suite of validated experience concepts addressing the highest-priority opportunity spaces identified in the journey. The concepts were designed with two interconnected goals: improving the overall quality and consistency of the member experience, and driving regular app engagement as a concrete retention behaviour — given that app log-in frequency was one of the strongest indicators of member retention.
Left: Final CX journey map. Right: Selected design concept mockups.
07

Design Concepts

Six concepts developed to drive first app login and consistent return engagement among the at-risk member segment. Each is grounded in the persona and the three experience principles. Concepts 1, 3 and 5 are considered highest priority — directly addressing the first login barrier, the life-stage trigger and the return habit.

Concept 01 — Priority
'Your Super So Far' — First Login Moment
High
Member Insight
"The persona doesn't know super is happening for them. Making the invisible visible creates an immediate emotional hook."
On first app open, instead of a generic dashboard, the member sees a personalised summary of what has already been accumulated on their behalf — framed as a positive surprise. "You've already saved $X without doing anything. Here's what that could look like at 65." Requires no financial literacy to understand, and directly embodies the surprising principle. Answers the unspoken question — "is this worth my time?" — in the first 30 seconds.
Concept 02
One-Question Onboarding Personaliser
Member Insight
"The persona has limited headspace. Generic onboarding reads as irrelevant before it's finished."
Rather than a full profile setup, ask a single question on first login: "What matters most to you right now?" with options like saving for something, understanding my money, or just getting set up. The answer tailor what the home screen surfaces — signalling that the experience is built around the member, not a generic template. The response also feeds data to personalise future communications.
Concept 03 — Priority
Life Milestone Triggers
High
Member Insight
"The persona is in a life transition. Meeting her at that moment with something relevant — not a generic marketing message — is exactly what 'surprisingly simple' means in practice."
Proactively reach out at moments the fund can reasonably infer — starting a new job, turning 21, receiving a first payslip above a threshold. A short, warm notification: "Looks like things are picking up for you. Here's one thing worth knowing about your super right now." Creates a reason to open the app that feels personal rather than promotional, and maps directly to the job change stage of the journey.
Concept 04
Progress Indicator with Dollar Value
Member Insight
"The persona's barrier is apathy, not hostility. Abstract future benefits don't move her; concrete personalised numbers do."
A subtle but persistent home screen element showing what completing a profile or reviewing an investment option could mean in dollar terms over time — not a nag, but a quiet and always-available nudge. Also gives the member a reason to return, since the figure changes as her balance grows. Respects attention while still driving action.
Concept 05 — Priority
'Just One Thing' Return Mechanic
High
Member Insight
"Consistent return behaviour is built on low friction. If every visit feels manageable, the habit forms. If every visit feels like homework, it doesn't."
Each time the member opens the app, surface one single relevant action or piece of information — never more. "This week: check your insurance cover. Takes 2 minutes." This is the intuitive principle applied to re-engagement — the app earns the next visit by not overloading the current one. Directly addresses the strongest behavioural retention signal identified in the data.
Concept 06
Social Proof for the Confidence Problem
Member Insight
"The persona's churn risk is partly identity-based: she perceives the fund as something she has outgrown."
Subtly surface proof points that normalise staying with the fund as members level up: "Members who joined at your age and stayed through their first job change have on average X% more saved by 30." Reframes the fund as something serious, engaged people actively choose to stay with — directly addressing the starter-fund perception in context, in the app, at the right moment.
08

Reflection

Inconsistency is often invisible to the teams living inside a product.
Each touchpoint had been designed and maintained by different people at different times — each one reasonable in isolation. But from a member's perspective, the cumulative effect was disorienting. Getting the client's team to see their own experience through that lens was as important as any of the design work itself.
A framework only has value if people can use it after you leave.
The experience framework was practical by design — it needed to be something the client could actually use after we left, not just a polished deliverable that sat in a folder. Building it with that in mind shaped how we structured the work and how we ran the workshops, making sure the internal team understood the reasoning behind it, not just the output.
Working across strategy, design and facilitation produces better work.
The strategy informs the design, the design stress-tests the strategy, and the facilitation makes sure the client is with you at each step rather than just receiving a final presentation. The cost is personal — it's demanding to switch modes constantly — but the quality of the output reflects it.
Test assumptions, not solutions.
One concept that had performed strongly in internal reviews was substantially de-prioritised after testing revealed it addressed a problem members didn't actually experience as painful. Framing usability sessions around behaviours and mental models rather than feature validation gave us richer insight and surfaced two major pivots before we'd committed to any direction.
